## What is Ketamine?

Ketamine is a dissociative anesthetic medication primarily used in medical settings. Developed in the 1960s, it has been employed for pain relief, sedation, and treatment-resistant depression. In its pure form, ketamine hydrochloride is a white crystalline powder or clear liquid. It's classified as a Schedule III controlled substance in many countries, meaning it has accepted medical uses but also potential for abuse.

Ketamine works by blocking NMDA receptors in the brain, leading to analgesic and hallucinogenic effects. Medically, it's administered via injection or infusion in hospitals. Off-label, low-dose ketamine therapy has gained popularity for mental health conditions like PTSD and anxiety.

### Medical Applications of Ketamine

- **Anesthesia:** Used in emergency medicine and veterinary practice due to its rapid onset and minimal respiratory depression.
- **Depression Treatment:** Esketamine (a nasal spray form) was FDA-approved in 2019 for treatment-resistant depression. Clinics offer ketamine infusions for mood disorders.
- **Pain Management:** Effective for chronic pain syndromes, such as fibromyalgia or neuropathic pain.

Research in 2026 continues to explore ketamine's role in neuroplasticity, potentially revolutionizing mental health care.

## Legal Status and Risks of Ketamine

Ketamine's legality varies globally. In the US, it's Schedule III under the DEA, legal with a prescription but illegal to possess or distribute without one. In the EU, it's controlled under similar frameworks. Illicit use often involves diversion from medical supplies or clandestine production.

### Potential Health Risks

Ketamine use carries significant dangers:
- **Short-term Effects:** Dissociation, elevated heart rate, nausea, and impaired coordination, increasing accident risk.
- **Long-term Risks:** Bladder damage (ketamine cystitis), cognitive impairments, and addiction potential.
- **Overdose:** Can lead to respiratory failure or psychosis, especially when mixed with other substances.

Harm reduction resources, like those from organizations such as DanceSafe, emphasize testing kits and moderation. If you're seeking treatment, reach out to licensed providers—never self-medicate.
